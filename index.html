FIELD NOTES FOR READING THE BOARD

RADAR REFLECTIVITY (dBZ)

Weather radar measures reflectivity — how much of the transmitted beam bounces back off precipitation — in decibels of Z (dBZ). Higher dBZ generally means heavier precipitation, though bright bands, hail, and beam artifacts can inflate values.

DBZROUGHLY MEANS
10–25Light rain, drizzle, flurries
25–40Moderate rain / steady snow
40–50Heavy rain, small hail possible
50+Severe storm cores, large hail likely

WATCH VS. WARNING

A watch means conditions are favorable — stay alert. A warning means the hazard is occurring or imminent — act now. On the map, warnings render hot (red), watches amber, advisories and statements yellow. Click any polygon for the full NWS alert text.

Intensity is only part of the story — storm surge, rainfall flooding, and forward speed often matter more than the category number.

READING A SURFACE CHART

On WPC surface charts: blue triangles mark cold fronts (pointing the direction of travel), red half-circles mark warm fronts, alternating symbols mark stationary fronts, and purple marks occlusions. An L is a low-pressure center — lift, clouds, weather; an H is a high — subsidence and generally quieter skies.

IR SATELLITE

Infrared imagery senses cloud-top temperature. Colder tops sit higher in the troposphere, so the brightest/coldest signatures usually flag deep convection — tall storms with heavy rain potential — and it works day and night.
