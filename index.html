NEW TO WEATHER MAPS? START HERE — NO EXPERIENCE NEEDED

SKYWATCH shows you what the sky is doing right now and what's coming next. You don't need to know any weather terms to use it — this page explains everything on the map in plain English. Skim the first section to get going, and dip into the rest whenever you see something you don't recognize.

START HERE — WHAT AM I LOOKING AT?

The main Map page is a live map of the weather. Here's everything you'll see on it:

  • ▚Colored blobs = rain, snow, or storms detected by radar. Green is light, yellow is moderate, orange is heavy, and red is a severe storm. Press the ▶ play button at the bottom to animate the last hour so you can see which way it's moving.
  • ▟Shaded outlines = official government weather alerts. Red is most serious, orange is a heads-up, yellow is minor. Click any outline to read exactly what it warns about and when it ends.
  • 72°Numbers scattered across the map = the current temperature (or wind) at ~50 cities. Use the TEMP / WIND / OFF buttons in the left panel to switch what they show.
  • ★The teal star = your saved favorite place. Type a town into the Favorite box on the left to track its weather and any alerts in one card.
  • 👆Click anywhere on the map to get a detailed forecast for that exact spot — right now, the next 48 hours, and the next 7 days.
Everything else below is optional. It explains why the weather does what it does, so the map tells you more each time you look.

THE COLORED BLOBS (RADAR)

In plain terms: radar is a machine that pings the sky with radio waves and listens for the echo bouncing back off raindrops, snowflakes, and hail. The stronger the echo, the more (or bigger) the drops — so warmer colors mean heavier precipitation. Scientists measure that echo strength in a unit called dBZ; you don't need the number, just the colors:

COLORWHAT IT USUALLY MEANS
GreenLight rain, drizzle, or snow flurries
YellowModerate rain or steady snow
OrangeHeavy rain — small hail possible
RedIntense storm core — large hail or damaging winds likely

Hit play to loop the recent frames. If the blobs are sliding toward you, the weather is on its way; if they're shrinking or fading, it's breaking up.

ALERTS: WHAT'S A "WATCH" VS A "WARNING"?

These two words sound similar but mean very different things. An easy way to remember it:

Watch = "watch out, it could happen." The ingredients are in place — keep an eye on the sky and have a plan.

Warning = "warning, it is happening (or about to)." Take action now to stay safe.

On the map, alerts are shaded by how serious they are: red for warnings, orange for watches, and yellow for minor advisories and statements. Click any shaded area to read the official wording, who issued it, and when it expires. Your favorite-place card on the left shows these too, and glows red when something is active.

WHAT'S A FRONT?

In plain terms: a front is the boundary where two big blobs of air — one warmer, one colder — bump into each other. Air masses don't mix easily, so along that dividing line you tend to get clouds, rain or storms, and a noticeable shift in temperature and wind. Fronts are the main reason your weather changes from day to day. On the Tropics + Fronts page you'll see them drawn as colored lines:

ON THE CHARTWHAT IT IS
Blue line, trianglesCold front — colder air pushing in. Often brief but punchy: showers or thunderstorms, then cooler, clearer air behind it.
Red line, half-circlesWarm front — warmer air sliding in. Usually a longer spell of clouds and steady, lighter rain, then milder air.
Purple lineOccluded front — a cold front catching up to a warm one; a mix of both, often near a storm's center.
Red & blue combinedStationary front — a boundary that's parked in place, so cloudy, unsettled weather can linger for days.

The little triangles or half-circles always point in the direction the front is moving.

HIGHS AND LOWS (THE BIG H AND L)

In plain terms: those letters mark centers of air pressure, and they're a quick shorthand for "nice" versus "unsettled."

H = High pressure. Air is sinking, which discourages clouds. Think calm, clear, settled weather.

L = Low pressure. Air is rising, which builds clouds and precipitation. Think wind, rain, and storms. Fronts usually trail out from an L.

THE "CLOUDS" LAYER (SATELLITE)

In plain terms: the satellite view shows where clouds are, day or night. It works by sensing temperature — cloud tops high up in the sky are very cold, and the coldest, brightest ones are the tallest, most towering storm clouds. So bright, cold cloud tops are a clue that heavy rain or thunderstorms may be underneath. Toggle it with the Clouds switch in the left panel.

HURRICANES AND TROPICAL STORMS

In plain terms: these are big spinning storms that form over warm ocean water. They're grouped by how fast their winds blow — the stronger the wind, the higher the category:

NAMETOP WIND SPEED
Tropical DepressionUp to 38 mph
Tropical Storm39–73 mph (gets a name)
Hurricane (Category 1–2)74–110 mph
Major Hurricane (Category 3–5)111 mph and up

Important: the category only measures wind. Most hurricane damage actually comes from water — ocean surge pushed ashore and inland flooding from heavy rain — so a "lower category" storm can still be very dangerous. The Tropics + Fronts page lists any active storms and shows the official outlook maps.

READING THE FORECAST NUMBERS

When you click the map or open your favorite place, a few terms show up:

FEELS LIKE
What the temperature actually feels like on your skin once humidity and wind are factored in — often more useful than the plain temperature.
HI / LO
The expected high and low temperature for the day.
WIND + ARROW
How fast the wind is blowing, in mph. The arrow points the way the wind is blowing toward.
RH (HUMIDITY)
How moist the air is, as a percentage. Higher numbers feel muggier and make hot days feel hotter.
